# The SAT Problem

Post 12 used SAT as a vehicle. I introduced it, ran through one formula, and kept moving because the point of that post was Cook-Levin. SAT was just the object Cook needed to prove his theorem about. We got enough of it to understand the theorem and nothing more.

This post is for SAT on its own terms.

When I actually sat down to understand it properly, not as a tool inside a larger proof but as a problem in its own right, a few things surprised me. The problem is simpler than it looks from the outside. The reason it is hard is also simpler than it looks. And the reason it became the go-to hard problem, the one everything else in $\mathcal{NP}$ reduces to, is not by accident. It is earned.

---

## What Post 12 Established

Post 12 introduced three operations: AND ($\wedge$), OR ($\vee$), NOT ($\neg$). It showed what a SAT formula looks like. The formula had Boolean variables (each either true or false), grouped into clauses (each clause is an OR of some variables, some possibly negated), and the whole thing was every clause joined by AND, meaning every single clause had to be true at the same time for the formula to count as satisfied.

Here is the example from Post 12:

$$\phi = (x_1 \vee \neg x_2) \wedge (\neg x_1 \vee x_3) \wedge (x_2 \vee \neg x_3)$$

Three variables. Three clauses. We found one assignment that works: $x_1 = \text{true}$, $x_2 = \text{true}$, $x_3 = \text{true}$.

That is the shape of SAT. The question is always the same: can you assign true or false to each variable so that the whole formula evaluates to true?

What Post 12 did not do was explain *why* this shape is the natural one, what makes it hard, or where it actually comes from. That is what this post is about.

---

## From Switches to Formulas

Something textbooks usually skip is that SAT is not just an abstract math object. It is the formal language of circuits.

Every digital circuit you have ever used, the processor in your phone, the chip in your laptop, the logic inside every program you have ever run, is built from three primitive components. You have probably seen their symbols your whole life without knowing what they were.

**AND gate.** Takes two inputs. Outputs true only when both inputs are true. Any other combination gives false. If you have a wire carrying signal A and a wire carrying signal B, an AND gate only outputs a live signal when both wires are live.

**OR gate.** Takes two inputs. Outputs true when at least one input is true. The output is only false when both inputs are false.

**NOT gate.** Takes one input and flips it. True becomes false. False becomes true. If a wire is live, the NOT gate kills it. If it is dead, the NOT gate makes it live.

That is it. Every computation that has ever happened on silicon is, at the circuit level, some mix of these three operations applied to binary signals.

:::info Why this matters for SAT
A SAT formula is just a description of a circuit. The variables $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ are the input wires. The AND, OR, NOT operations are the gates. The formula evaluates to true when the right combination of inputs makes the output wire live.

"Is this formula satisfiable?" is the same question as "Is there a setting of the input switches that makes this circuit output true?" Same problem, different notation. This is exactly what Cook showed in 1971: any computation, including any verification of any certificate for any problem in $\mathcal{NP}$, can be encoded as a circuit, and a circuit can be encoded as a SAT formula.
:::

## Building Up to CNF

Before I can explain CNF, I need to define one word that I should have defined in Post 12 but didn't: **literal**.

A literal is just a variable or a negated variable. That's the whole definition. $x_1$ is a literal. $\neg x_2$ is a literal. $x_3$ is a literal. $\neg x_3$ is a literal. Basically: a single variable, with or without a NOT in front of it.

I am defining this now because CNF is defined in terms of literals, and if I say "literal" without defining it first, the definition of CNF becomes meaningless.

Now, CNF.

**Conjunctive Normal Form** is a specific way of writing a Boolean formula. CNF means the formula looks like this: a bunch of groups, each group being a set of literals joined by OR, and all the groups joined by AND.

In plain words: it is AND of ORs.

$$\phi = \underbrace{(\text{literal} \vee \text{literal} \vee \ldots)}_{\text{clause}} \wedge \underbrace{(\text{literal} \vee \text{literal} \vee \ldots)}_{\text{clause}} \wedge \ldots$$

Each group is called a clause. Each clause is a bunch of literals ORed together. The whole formula is all the clauses ANDed together.

The formula from Post 12 is already in this shape:

$$\phi = \underbrace{(x_1 \vee \neg x_2)}_{\text{clause 1}} \wedge \underbrace{(\neg x_1 \vee x_3)}_{\text{clause 2}} \wedge \underbrace{(x_2 \vee \neg x_3)}_{\text{clause 3}}$$

Three clauses. Each clause is literals ORed together. The clauses are ANDed. That is CNF.

:::note Why CNF specifically?
SAT formulas can be written in all kinds of ways. You could have an OR of ANDs instead of an AND of ORs. You could have deeply nested expressions. Why does every textbook, every proof, and every SAT solver always use this specific AND-of-ORs structure?

The reason is that any Boolean formula, no matter how nested or complicated, can always be converted into this AND-of-ORs format. The converted version might be bigger, but it will be satisfiable if and only if the original was. So the CNF format does not throw away any information that matters. And because it works for everything, it became the one standard format that SAT solvers expect. You give a SAT solver a formula in CNF and it handles the rest.

I am not going to show how the conversion works because I honestly have not learned it in full detail yet. It involves something called the Tseitin transformation and I will come back to it when I understand it properly. For now the important thing is just knowing that the conversion is always possible, which is why CNF is the standard.
:::

## What Makes a Clause Fail

Here is the simplest way to think about what a SAT formula is asking.

Each clause is a requirement. It says: at least one of these literals must be true. A clause fails, meaning it becomes false, only when every single literal in it is false. Not most of them. Every single one.

This gives you a clear picture of what satisfying the formula actually means. You need every clause to pass at the same time. You are looking for one assignment of true/false to the variables that keeps all the requirements satisfied simultaneously.

When clauses push against each other, when satisfying one forces you to violate another, things get hard. The difficulty of SAT is exactly this: finding one assignment that threads through all the requirements at once without breaking any of them.

<Tabs>
  <TabItem value="easy" label="🟢 Constraints that agree" default>

$$\phi = (x_1) \wedge (x_1 \vee x_2) \wedge (x_1 \vee \neg x_2)$$

The first clause demands $x_1 = \text{true}$. Once that is set, the other two clauses are automatically satisfied because $(x_1 \vee \text{anything})$ is always true when $x_1$ is true. There is no conflict. The assignment $x_1 = \text{true}$ works regardless of what $x_2$ is.

The search space here is $2^2 = 4$ possible assignments, and the first one you try works.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="medium" label="🟡 Constraints that interact">

$$\phi = (x_1 \vee x_2) \wedge (\neg x_1 \vee x_3) \wedge (\neg x_2 \vee \neg x_3)$$

Clause 1 says at least one of $x_1$, $x_2$ must be true.
Clause 2 says if $x_1$ is true, then $x_3$ must also be true.
Clause 3 says $x_2$ and $x_3$ cannot both be true.

So if $x_1$ is true, clause 2 forces $x_3 = \text{true}$, which means clause 3 forces $x_2 = \text{false}$. Does this still satisfy clause 1? Yes, because $x_1$ is still true. The assignment $x_1 = \text{true}$, $x_2 = \text{false}$, $x_3 = \text{true}$ works.

Notice that you had to follow a chain of forced decisions before you could confirm it. That is what makes SAT interesting.

  </TabItem>
  <TabItem value="hard" label="🔴 Constraints that contradict">

$$\phi = (x_1) \wedge (\neg x_1)$$

Clause 1 demands $x_1 = \text{true}$.
Clause 2 demands $x_1 = \text{false}$.
No assignment can satisfy both. This formula is **unsatisfiable**.

For real SAT instances, this kind of contradiction is never this obvious. You might have thousands of variables and millions of clauses and the contradiction only becomes visible after you follow chains of forced decisions through hundreds of steps. Confirming unsatisfiability means confirming that every possible assignment eventually fails somewhere. That is a much harder thing to verify, and there is no short certificate you can hand over the way there is for a satisfying assignment. This asymmetry is something we will come back to when we look at the class $\mathcal{coNP}$ in Phase 3.

  </TabItem>
</Tabs>

## Encoding a Real Problem in SAT

Here is something I find genuinely cool about SAT. It is not just one specific problem about logic formulas. It is a language that can express almost any problem where you are asking whether a set of yes/no constraints can all be satisfied at once. Let me show you what that looks like with a small example that is easy to trace by hand.

**The problem.** Three tasks need to be scheduled. Each task gets assigned exactly one time slot: morning or afternoon. Two constraints apply:

1. Task 1 and Task 2 cannot run at the same time. They share a resource.
2. Task 2 and Task 3 cannot run at the same time. They also share a resource.

Is there a valid schedule?

**The encoding.**

We create a Boolean variable for each task. Let $x_i = \text{true}$ mean Task $i$ is assigned to morning, and $x_i = \text{false}$ mean Task $i$ is assigned to afternoon.

Constraint 1 says Task 1 and Task 2 cannot both be morning and cannot both be afternoon. In terms of our variables:

Not both morning means at least one of them is NOT in the morning, which is the clause $(\neg x_1 \vee \neg x_2)$.

Not both afternoon means at least one of them IS in the morning, which is the clause $(x_1 \vee x_2)$.

Wait, let me be precise about that second one. Both being afternoon means $x_1 = \text{false}$ and $x_2 = \text{false}$ both at once. Saying that is NOT allowed is the same as saying $x_1 = \text{true}$ OR $x_2 = \text{true}$. So the clause is $(x_1 \vee x_2)$.

Constraint 2 gives the same two clauses for Task 2 and Task 3: $(\neg x_2 \vee \neg x_3)$ and $(x_2 \vee x_3)$.

The full formula:

$$\phi = (x_1 \vee x_2) \wedge (\neg x_1 \vee \neg x_2) \wedge (x_2 \vee x_3) \wedge (\neg x_2 \vee \neg x_3)$$

Now try $x_1 = \text{true}$, $x_2 = \text{false}$, $x_3 = \text{true}$ and check each clause:

- $(x_1 \vee x_2) = (\text{true} \vee \text{false}) = \text{true}$ ✓
- $(\neg x_1 \vee \neg x_2) = (\text{false} \vee \text{true}) = \text{true}$ ✓
- $(x_2 \vee x_3) = (\text{false} \vee \text{true}) = \text{true}$ ✓
- $(\neg x_2 \vee \neg x_3) = (\text{true} \vee \text{false}) = \text{true}$ ✓

The formula is satisfied. The valid schedule is: Task 1 in the morning, Task 2 in the afternoon, Task 3 in the morning.

:::tip What this shows
This scheduling problem became a SAT formula. The same thing works for graph coloring, where variables represent color assignments. It works for circuit verification, where variables represent wire states. It works for planning problems, timetabling, and hundreds of other things. SAT is not one narrow problem. It is a general language for expressing any situation where you are asking "can all these requirements be satisfied at once?" This is why Cook chose it as the thing to prove NP-Completeness about. Everything reduces to it because everything can be expressed in it.
:::

## The Easy/Hard Split

Not all SAT instances are equally hard. I found this surprising when I first looked into it, and it is worth understanding before we move to 3-SAT in Post 14.

**2-SAT is in $\mathcal{P}$.**

If every clause contains exactly two literals, the problem is called 2-SAT. There is a polynomial-time algorithm that solves it. The algorithm converts the formula into a kind of directed graph and analyzes its structure. I have not learned the full details yet so I will not pretend to explain it properly here. But the key fact is: 2-SAT has a real polynomial-time solution. Restricting every clause to exactly two literals makes the problem tractable in a way that three literals does not.

**3-SAT is NP-Complete.**

If every clause contains exactly three literals, you get 3-SAT. Post 14 will prove this is NP-Complete through a reduction from SAT. The jump from two literals per clause to three is not just a small size increase. Something structurally changes. With three literals per clause, you can express constraints that two literals per clause cannot express, and that extra expressiveness is exactly what brings the full difficulty of NP back in. Post 14 addresses the exact reason.

**The phase transition in random 3-SAT.**

Here is something I found that researchers discovered by running experiments. If you generate random 3-SAT formulas and vary the ratio of clauses to variables, something striking happens.

When there are very few clauses relative to the number of variables, the formula is almost always satisfiable. There is so much freedom in assigning variables that nearly any assignment works.

When there are very many clauses relative to variables, the formula is almost always unsatisfiable. The constraints pile up and contradict each other.

But right around a specific ratio (around 4.27 clauses per variable for 3-SAT), the formula flips sharply from almost always satisfiable to almost always unsatisfiable. And the hardest instances for real SAT solvers cluster right at this boundary. Not because anyone designed them to be hard, just because that is where the constraints are tight enough to resist easy solution but not so overwhelming that contradictions are obvious.

This is one of the most actively studied phenomena in SAT research and I find it genuinely fascinating. The boundary is not well understood theoretically, but computationally you can see it clearly in experiments.

## Why SAT Is Hard

The difficulty has a clean explanation that does not require any formal machinery.

Each variable has two choices: true or false. With $n$ variables, there are $2^n$ possible assignments. For $n = 50$, that is over $10^{15}$ assignments. For $n = 300$, which is a small industrial SAT instance, the number of assignments is larger than the number of atoms in the observable universe.

You cannot check them all. Any algorithm that works by trying every assignment is dead on arrival.

Can you do better than checking every assignment? This is where it gets interesting. Modern SAT solvers, programs like MiniSat and Glucose, are genuinely powerful. They solve formulas with millions of variables in seconds by using smart techniques: they propagate forced assignments, they learn from failed branches, they backtrack non-chronologically. They are not doing brute force.

But no one has found a polynomial-time algorithm for SAT. Every solver eventually hits instances where the search explodes. And the theoretical wall is exactly what Cook proved: if you found a polynomial-time algorithm for SAT, you would have $\mathcal{P} = \mathcal{NP}$. Every problem in $\mathcal{NP}$ would become efficiently solvable. All of modern cryptography would collapse overnight. The belief that this cannot happen is what makes SAT hard to doubt.

:::note The two-sided mystery
We do not know of a polynomial-time SAT algorithm. We also cannot prove that no such algorithm exists. SAT sits exactly at the line where our tools run out. It is in $\mathcal{NP}$ because a satisfying assignment is a certificate that can be checked in polynomial time. It is NP-Hard because everything in $\mathcal{NP}$ reduces to it. Whether it is in $\mathcal{P}$ is the $\mathcal{P}$ vs $\mathcal{NP}$ question itself.
:::

## SAT as a Common Language

There is a way of thinking about SAT that I have not seen written this way anywhere else, but it is the thing that made it click for me.

SAT is not one problem among many. It is a shared language that every hard problem in $\mathcal{NP}$ can be translated into. When you reduce graph coloring to SAT, you are not doing something specific to graph coloring. You are translating graph coloring into the same language as scheduling, and circuit verification, and protein folding, and Sudoku. All of these problems, when reduced to SAT, are asking the same underlying question: can these requirements all be satisfied at once?

That is not just a complexity theory insight. It is something deeper about what makes problems hard. They are hard for the same reason. The difficulty is the same difficulty, just wearing different clothes.

Post 14 takes the next step. 3-SAT is the version of SAT with every clause restricted to exactly three literals, and the reduction from SAT to 3-SAT is one of the cleanest reductions in the series. It will show you how to reshape a formula while keeping exactly the information that matters.
